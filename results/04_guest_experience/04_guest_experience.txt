=============================================
Generated from analysis in results/04_guest_experience/ (see q*_summary.md and CSVs per question).


Q1. What do guests complain about most in reviews? Does this differ by city or property type?
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Answer:
  We measure “complaint friction” as the share of review comments (comments_clean)
  that match a fixed set of problem-oriented cues: noise, smell, cleanliness and
  breakage issues, delays, disappointment, etc. (regex scan; one chunked pass over
  reviews joined to master_data for City and property_type).

  By city, Hawaii has the highest share of in-scope reviews with at least one such
  cue (~20.1%). Ordering by city (highest to lowest cue share): Hawaii, New York,
  San Francisco, Nashville, Los Angeles.

  By property type, rare types can show extreme rates from tiny samples. Among
  property types with at least 500 in-scope reviews, “Room in hotel” shows the
  highest complaint-cue share (~34.0%). Hotel and serviced-apartment–style room
  types generally rank high in the full table.

  Interpretation: higher shares mean more problem-oriented language in text, not
  necessarily worse operations without checking structured scores and segment mix.

Q2. What guest experience factors (cleanliness, check-in, communication, location)
    matter most for earning high overall reviews?
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Answer:
  Outcome: is_high_overall = 1 if review_scores_rating >= 4.9 (~49.7% of listings
  with non-missing sub-scores in the modeling sample).

  Predictors: Airbnb sub-scores — cleanliness, check-in, communication, location.

  Both logistic regression and a random forest (train/val/test split 70/15/15,
  RANDOM_STATE=42) point to the same story:
  - Cleanliness is the strongest predictor (largest logistic coefficient; highest
    random forest feature importance, ~43%).
  - Communication is second (importance ~26%; logistic coef similar in magnitude
    to cleanliness).
  - Check-in and location matter but rank third and fourth in this snapshot.

  Test AUC for logistic regression is about 0.91. Sub-scores are correlated;
  results are associative, not independent causal effects.

Supporting outputs:
  results/04_guest_experience/q2_five_star_drivers/q2_subscore_importance_ranking.csv
  results/04_guest_experience/q2_five_star_drivers/q2_subscore_correlation_with_overall.csv


Q3. Are there specific operational investments (e.g., self-check-in, professional
    cleaning) that the review data suggests would pay off?
---------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Answer:
  We proxy “investments” with flags parsed from the amenities string (e.g. self
  check-in wording, lockbox/smart lock/keypad, mentions of cleaning) plus
  instant_bookable, then compare mean review_scores_rating and share of listings
  with overall rating >= 4.9. This is descriptive association only (hosts who
  list more amenities may differ on many dimensions).

  In this snapshot, listings with lockbox/keypad/smart-lock style language show
  the highest mean overall rating among flags examined; cleaning-related amenity
  mentions also align with higher averages. Self check-in wording is positive but
  slightly lower on mean rating than lockbox/cleaning in the flag=True rows.
  Instant Book listings here show lower mean overall ratings — treat as segment
  mix, not proof that Instant Book hurts guests.

Supporting outputs:
  results/04_guest_experience/q3_operational_investments/q3_operational_signal_assoc.csv


Q4. What do guests praise in top-performing listings that the client should replicate?
------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Answer:
  Top performers = top quartile of review_scores_rating (threshold about 4.97 in
  this data). We compare listing–city TF-IDF deviation terms (words that
  over-index vs same-city peers) between top quartile and others.

  Terms that lift among top listings include hospitality and repeat-stay language
  (stems after Porter): e.g. recommend, again, friendli, experi, back, world,
  approach, bay — i.e. strong endorsement and “would return” style signals more
  than a single amenity name.

  Caveat: deviations highlight distinctive language vs local peers, not guaranteed
  “praise only”; validate themes qualitatively before standardizing host messaging.

Supporting outputs:
  results/04_guest_experience/q4_top_performer_praise/q4_term_lift_top_vs_other.csv
  results/04_guest_experience/text_features/listing_city_deviation_terms.csv
